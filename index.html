MBTA Commuter Rail Analysis

This project explores patterns in MBTA commuter rail usage to better understand how public transportation is utilized across the Boston area.

By Fifi, Samantha, Carolyn

Project Overview

This project explores patterns in MBTA commuter rail usage to better understand how public transportation is utilized across the Boston area. By analyzing ridership data across routes, time periods, and service types, we aim to identify meaningful trends in passenger behavior. These patterns provide insight into when and where demand is highest, as well as how ridership varies over time.

Understanding these dynamics is important as Boston continues to grow and evolve. Changes in ridership can reflect broader societal trends such as shifts toward remote work, population movement, and environmental awareness. By examining these patterns through data visualization, this project highlights opportunities for improving transit efficiency, reducing congestion, and encouraging greater use of public transportation.

About the Data

The dataset used in this project comes from the MBTA Open Data Portal and contains detailed information about commuter rail ridership in the Boston area. After cleaning, the dataset includes over 15,000 observations and more than 10 attributes. Key variables include route name, stop time, date, day type, and measures of passenger activity such as average boardings, alightings, and total passenger load.

This dataset includes both categorical and continuous variables, allowing us to analyze patterns across routes as well as changes over time. The data was preprocessed by removing duplicates and missing values, standardizing route names, and creating additional variables such as hour and total passengers. While the dataset provides strong coverage of ridership behavior, it does not include external factors such as service delays or weather conditions, which may also influence trends.

1. Ridership Trends Over Time

This visualization highlights how average passenger boardings have changed over time across different commuter rail routes. It allows us to compare long-term ridership trends and identify which routes maintain consistently high usage. Some routes show stable demand over time, while others display more variability, suggesting differences in commuter behavior and route importance within the system.

2. Average Passengers On Board by Hour and Route

This heatmap shows how passenger load varies throughout the day across different routes. Darker regions indicate higher concentrations of passengers, revealing peak travel periods and heavily used routes. The visualization highlights clear time-based patterns in ridership, particularly during commuting hours, and helps identify when demand is most concentrated.

3. Most Consistent Routes

This chart compares the consistency of different routes using the standard deviation of passenger load. Routes with lower variation tend to have more stable and predictable ridership patterns, while higher variation indicates fluctuations in demand. This helps identify which routes operate with steady usage and which may experience more irregular traffic.

4. Passenger Distribution by Hour

This D3 visualization shows how total passenger activity is distributed across different hours of the day. It highlights clear peak periods when ridership is highest, as well as lower-demand hours. The interactive design allows users to explore exact passenger values, providing a more detailed understanding of daily travel patterns.

5. Passenger Flow: Day Type to Route to Time of Day

This Sankey diagram illustrates how passenger volume flows from day types (weekday vs. weekend) into major commuter rail routes and then into different times of day. It provides a high-level view of how ridership is distributed across the system, showing how travel behavior changes depending on both schedule and route usage.

Summary of Findings

Overall, the visualizations reveal that MBTA commuter rail ridership is strongly influenced by both time and route. Certain routes consistently attract higher passenger volumes, while others show more variability. Daily patterns indicate clear peak commuting hours, with demand concentrated during specific time periods. Additionally, differences between weekday and weekend travel highlight how commuter behavior changes depending on purpose and schedule.

These findings suggest opportunities for improving transit planning and resource allocation. For example, increasing service during peak hours or optimizing underutilized routes could improve efficiency. In the future, this analysis could be expanded by incorporating additional factors such as weather, service delays, or seasonal trends to gain a deeper understanding of ridership behavior.
